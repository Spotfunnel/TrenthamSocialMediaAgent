## 1. Brand Identity Statement

Trentham Electrical & Solar is a locally owned and operated solar and electrical business based in the small town of Trentham, Victoria, in the heart of the Macedon Ranges and Hepburn Shire region. The business is led by Mick (full name Mick McDonald), a Master Electrician with over 20 years of experience. The company designs, installs, and supports grid-connected solar, off-grid solar, battery storage, EV charging, heat pump hot water systems, and HVAC across the Central Highlands of regional Victoria. They are Clean Energy Council (CEC) certified system designers and installers, and SAA accredited. The team consists of named individuals -- Lewis, Ritchie, Sam, Kevin, Max, and Grant -- who are regularly celebrated in content. The business operates from a fleet of distinctive purple-wrapped vans that have become a visual icon in the region.

The brand occupies the position of "local trusted expert" -- not premium-luxury, not budget. Trentham Electrical & Solar is the neighbour who happens to be a highly qualified electrician: someone who answers the phone, explains things clearly, recommends the right-sized system (even if it is smaller than what was requested), and follows up after the job is done. The business is deeply embedded in the communities it serves, sponsoring local organisations, installing systems at schools and childcare centres, and naming specific towns, streets, and local businesses in its content. When a customer in Trentham sees the purple van drive past, they wave -- that is the relationship this brand cultivates.

The brand's competitive advantage, confirmed repeatedly in customer reviews, rests on three pillars: responsive communication ("Mick is only ever a text or call away"), meticulous workmanship ("the overall finish is extremely tidy"), and genuine after-service support ("the service did not end at installation"). Content produced for this brand must consistently reinforce these three pillars while maintaining the warm, knowledgeable, unpretentious voice that defines Trentham Electrical & Solar.

## 2. Voice & Personality

The brand personality is captured by five defining adjectives. Each is explained below with verbatim examples and guidance on intensity.

### 2.1 PROUD

**What it means:** Genuine, earned pride in completed work, the team, and community impact. Every install is treated as something worth celebrating -- not routine. Pride extends to the team members, the region, and the quality of the finished product.

**How it sounds:**
- "We're absolutely stoked to share some exciting news -- Trentham Electrical & Solar has won @aikoenergy Best Residential Installation!"
- "Another future-proof install we're proud to hand over."
- "Our crew put real care into every detail -- from the layout of the panels to the tidy wiring behind the scenes."
- "It's that mix of craftsmanship and pride that makes our installs stand out."
- "We would not allow anyone else to manage or modify the completed works as the outcome is stellar." (customer review, echoing brand values)

**Dial up:** Install showcases, award announcements, team profiles, milestone posts.
**Dial down:** Educational content, rebate explainers, customer-problem-focused posts. Pride should never overshadow the reader's needs.

### 2.2 LOCAL

**What it means:** Regional identity is core, not cosmetic. The brand is not a Melbourne company servicing the regions -- Mick and the team live and work in these towns. Local knowledge (climate, grid conditions, Powercor outage patterns) informs system design. Content name-drops specific suburbs, landmarks, and local businesses naturally.

**How it sounds:**
- "From Daylesford to Woodend and right here in Trentham, we design systems built for the Central Highlands climate."
- "At Trentham Electrical & Solar, we're your neighbours -- not just your installers."
- "When you choose us, you're not just investing in solar -- you're supporting local jobs, genuine expertise, and a team that cares."
- "Hepburn Shire's largest solar retailer and installer."
- "A long-time local to Trentham/Hepburn Shire, Max came from a background in small farming and hospitality."

**Dial up:** Community project posts, team introductions, posts featuring specific towns, brand philosophy content.
**Dial down:** Product spotlights and technical explainers (local references can still appear, but should not dominate).

### 2.3 TRUSTWORTHY

**What it means:** Integrity is demonstrated, not just claimed. The brand proves trustworthiness by recommending right-sized systems (even smaller ones), explaining design rationale, being transparent about challenges, and never using high-pressure sales tactics. "No shortcuts" is a recurring theme.

**How it sounds:**
- "We take the time to do it properly -- no shortcuts, no dodgy finishes."
- "The data we care about has nothing to do with sales, but everything to do with your power usage."
- "No hard sell, no fluff -- just solid, well-designed solar that works."
- "We don't just install solar -- we guide you through the rebate process to make sure you get every dollar you're entitled to."
- "No pressure, no BS. Just honest advice from locals who've been doing this work across Central Victoria for years."

**Dial up:** Posts about the design process, rebate guidance, value proposition content, responses to common concerns.
**Dial down:** Celebratory posts, team fun content. Trust is always present in the background but does not need to be the headline of every post.

### 2.4 KNOWLEDGEABLE

**What it means:** Technical confidence without arrogance. Mick's 20+ years of experience and Master Electrician status underpin the brand's authority. Complex topics (STCs, DC vs AC coupling, shading algorithms, three-phase systems) are explained clearly. The brand teaches rather than lectures.

**How it sounds:**
- "Modern panels work in the shade and overcast at a reduced capacity, and modern inverters have algorithms to deal with shading."
- "A system in Trentham generates more power on a sunny day than a sunny day in Alice Springs."
- "DC hybrids connect solar panels and batteries directly to the inverter. During a power outage, a DC hybrid system will continue to generate electricity."
- "The Victorian government's battery rebate isn't a straightforward cash-back deal. Instead, it works through something called Small-scale Technology Certificates (STCs). Think of STCs as credits that get assigned to your battery system."
- "Mick demonstrated detailed, in-depth knowledge of both the hardware and software, which gave me real confidence." (customer review)

**Dial up:** Blog posts, educational content, product spotlights, rebate explainers.
**Dial down:** Community celebration posts, casual team content. Even when dialled down, never say anything technically inaccurate.

### 2.5 WARM

**What it means:** The tone is approachable, friendly, and genuine. Team members are introduced by name with personal details. Customers are referenced by first name. The purple van is treated like a family mascot. The brand sounds like a skilled tradie explaining things over a coffee, not a corporation issuing a press release.

**How it sounds:**
- "We love being part of projects that invest in our community and our kids."
- "We're happy to have a down-to-earth chat about how storage works for your property."
- "Stay powered, Mick & the crew."
- "If you've been thinking about solar, now's a brilliant time to take the leap."
- "Thinking about solar for your home? Let's chat."

**Dial up:** Team profiles, community posts, customer interaction, CTAs.
**Dial down:** Formal technical content, compliance-related messaging. Even at its most formal, the brand should never sound cold.

### 3.2 "Never Use" List

| Word/Phrase | Why It Is Off-Brand |
|-------------|---------------------|
| "cheap" / "budget" / "affordable" (to describe own work) | Brand competes on quality, not price. "Competitive" or "fair" are acceptable. |
| "Limited time only!" / "Act now or miss out!" | Aggressive scarcity tactics contradict the no-pressure ethos. Even rebate deadlines are communicated factually. |
| "disruption" / "revolutionary" / Silicon Valley jargon | The brand is grounded, practical, and regional. Not a tech startup. |
| "guarantee" (used loosely) | Only use when referring to actual manufacturer warranties or specific guarantees. |
| "we're the best" / superlatives without evidence | Let awards, reviews, and work quality speak. Never self-declare superiority. |
| Competitor names | Never name a competitor. Differentiate through implied quality: "We've seen some horror stories from homeowners who went with out-of-town installers." |
| "customers" / "clients" / "leads" (in public-facing content) | Address people as "you," "homeowners," "neighbours," or by first name. |
| "heaps good" / excessive slang | One or two Australian colloquialisms per post maximum. The brand is casual-professional, not larrikin. |
| Price-matching / discounting language | The brand does not compete on price. Never mention discounts, price-matching, or cheapest-in-market claims. |
| Political language or controversy | The brand is apolitical. Renewable energy is framed as practical and financial, not political. |
| "just" (as a minimiser) | Avoid "we just install solar" -- the brand takes pride in its work. "Just" undercuts that. Exception: "just honest advice." |
| "synergy" / "leverage" / "stakeholder" | Corporate jargon. Completely off-brand. |
| "eco-warrior" / "save the planet" | Environmental benefit is secondary and understated. Never preachy or activist. |

## 4. Tone Spectrum

### 4.1 Where the Brand Sits

```
Formal --------|--------X----|------------ Casual
                        ^
              Casual-professional

Sales ----------|----X-------|------------ Educational
                      ^
           Education-first, soft sell

Corporate ------|------------|---X-------- Personal
                                  ^
                        Personal-team voice
```

### 4.2 The Typical Range (with examples)

**Most formal the brand ever gets** (still accessible):
> "With extensive experience across complex commercial installs, we understand high energy loads, three-phase systems, compliance requirements and minimising downtime during installation."

> "When contemplating a generator for an off-grid solar property, it's crucial to evaluate the energy needs, the required duration of backup, and the compatibility of the generator."

**Standard mid-range** (the sweet spot -- 70% of content):
> "Every system we install is designed to quietly do its job in the background: soaking up sunshine, lowering costs, and making day-to-day life run a little smoother."

> "Going solar is a smart investment -- but only if it's done right. A quality solar installer will design a system tailored to your roof, your energy needs, and your long-term goals."

> "There's something extra satisfying about seeing a powerful solar system on this beautiful white home with a sparkling pool."

**Most casual the brand gets** (still professional):
> "We can't roo-sist sharing this one..."

> "If rising power bills are doing your head in..."

> "hello pool pumps and summer air con!"

> "Should call them LONG Blacks!" (coffee roasters install with LONGi panels)

**THE BOUNDARY -- never cross these lines:**
- Never so formal it sounds like a government report or corporate press release
- Never so casual it sounds sloppy, unprofessional, or like clickbait
- Never aggressive, confrontational, or negative toward anyone
- Never ironic, sarcastic, or cynical

## 5. How We Talk About...

### 5.1 Customers

**Always:** Address as "you" and "your" in direct content. When referencing specific customers, use first names: "Debbie and her family," "Heather from Trentham," "Pricilla." Customers are framed as community members and neighbours, never as transactions.

**Never:** Use "customers," "clients," or "leads" in public-facing content. Never share surnames, exact addresses, or financial details without explicit permission.

**Pattern:** "We recently had a customer in Trentham who initially requested a 13 kW solar system. But when we examined their energy usage, we had some questions." -- This shows the advisory relationship.

### 5.2 The Region

**Name-dropping is core to the brand.** The following towns and areas should be referenced regularly and rotated through content:

**Primary (home base):** Trentham
**Inner ring (most frequently mentioned):** Daylesford, Kyneton, Woodend, Creswick, Blackwood, Lyonville
**Outer ring (mentioned regularly):** Macedon, Glenlyon, Clunes, Musk, Tylden, Lancefield, Gisborne, Bacchus Marsh, Eganstown, Hepburn Springs, Malmsbury, Smeaton
**Regional identifiers:** Macedon Ranges, Hepburn Shire, Central Highlands, Central Victoria, Regional Victoria, "cool country"

**Pattern:** "From Daylesford to Woodend and right here in Trentham, we design systems built for the Central Highlands climate." Geographic references are woven into narratives, never listed artificially.

**Local businesses mentioned:** Sisko Chocolate, Martin Street Coffee Roasters, Trentham Golf Club, Macedon Primary School, Daylesford Community Child Care Centre, Hepburn Springs Brewing Co, Trentham General (cafe). Reference these when showcasing local projects.

### 5.3 Money & Value

**The cardinal rule: never lead with price.** Value is framed as long-term investment, savings over time, and energy independence.

**Approved framing:**
- "It's not just about cutting bills. It's about future-proofing your home."
- "A very savvy investment that will pay itself off in no time."
- "Delivering a massive 675% ROI -- proof that solar isn't just good for the planet, it's great for business."
- "Estimated annual savings: $1,726.91" (specific savings are fine when tied to a real system)
- "We guide you through the rebate process to make sure you get every dollar you're entitled to."

**Rebate/incentive language:** Be factual and specific. State dollar amounts, deadlines, and eligibility clearly. Create urgency through facts, not hype: "The difference between installing in April versus June could cost you several thousand dollars in lost rebate value."

**Never:** Lead a post with a price point. Use "cheap," "budget," or "discount." Frame solar as a bargain purchase. Mention price-matching.

### 5.4 Competition

**Never name a competitor.** Differentiate through implied quality and cautionary references to bad experiences:
- "We've seen some horror stories from homeowners who went with out-of-town installers chasing cheap prices. They got dodgy installs, batteries that don't work properly, and no one to call when things go wrong."
- "No cutting corners, no 'she'll be right' jobs."
- "Unlike some low-cost options in the market, our systems are built to perform reliably, year after year."

**Pattern:** Frame the competitor risk without naming anyone. Emphasise what the customer gains from choosing local and quality, not what they lose from choosing others.

### 5.5 The Team

**Named individuals create the "real people" feeling that separates this brand from faceless installers:**
- **Mick** -- Owner, Master Electrician, 20+ years experience. The face of the brand. Big beard, cap, branded shirt. Referenced as the design and consultation lead.
- **Kevin** -- Business Development Manager. Handles technical setup, app configuration, battery programming. Featured in post-install support.
- **Lewis** -- Electrician/apprentice. Featured working on roofs. Younger team member.
- **Sam** -- A-Grade Electrician. "Down-to-earth," loves keeping active.
- **Ritchie** -- Electrician. Featured in dedicated intro post.
- **Max** -- Apprentice. Local to Trentham, background in farming and hospitality.
- **Grant** -- Team member referenced in content.

**Language for the team:** Use craftsmanship and trade-pride language: "the crew," "our team," "the lads." Celebrate early starts, steep roofs, heavy battery lifts. Frame the work as skilled and physical: "Every solar install, every early start, every steep roof and heavy battery lift -- it all comes down to people backing each other in."

**Sign-offs that reference the team:** "Stay powered, Mick & the crew." / "Warm wishes, The Trentham Electrical and Solar Team."

### 5.6 The Environment & Sustainability

**Sustainability is an understated, secondary benefit.** It is never the headline. The primary pitch is always financial (savings, ROI, independence) or practical (backup power, reliability). Environmental benefit is the quiet bonus.

**Approved framing:**
- "Clean energy" / "clean, green energy" (simple, understated)
- "Woodend Goes Green" (as a celebration, not a lecture)
- "You're not just investing in solar -- you're supporting local jobs, genuine expertise, and a team that cares."
- Seedling/leaf emoji used sparingly for sustainability content

**Never:** Be preachy, activist, or political about climate change. Never use "eco-warrior," "save the planet," or guilt-based environmental messaging. The brand's customers are motivated by savings and independence first, environmental benefit second.

### 5.7 Technology & Products

**The brand is confident and educational about technology, not worshipful.**

**Current product partners (as of early 2026):**
- **Inverters:** Fronius (GEN24, Primo, SYMO, Verto, Reserva), Victron Energy (off-grid), Sigenergy, GoodWe
- **Panels:** AIKO Energy (current primary), LONGi (All Black, HiMO6), Canadian Solar
- **Batteries:** BYD (Premium), Sigenergy, FranklinWH, Victron
- **Other:** iStore heat pumps, Fronius Wattpilot EV charger, Kubota generators, Clenergy racking

**Brand-naming pattern:** Always name the product brand when listing specs. Use the manufacturer's preferred capitalisation. Tag the brand's social accounts where relevant. On Instagram, hashtag product brands. On GBP, no hashtags.

**How to describe products:**
- Lead with what it does for the customer, then name the product: "When it comes to reliable, high-performance home energy storage, the Fronius Reserva is quickly becoming the standout favourite across our installs."
- "The team was seriously impressed with the Fronius Verto's capabilities."
- "Victron Energy is renowned for its dominance in off-grid solar installations worldwide, known for its reliability and adaptability."

**Product transitions:** The brand has transitioned panel brands over time (Jinko to LONGi to AIKO). When introducing new products, narrate the transition positively: "Notice anything different? Trentham Electrical have moved to the aesthetically awesome LONGi 430W HiMO6 All Black as our standard solar panels."

## 6. Emotional Palette

### 6.1 PRIDE (dominant -- present in 80%+ of posts)

The most frequently used emotion. Pride appears as celebration of completed work, team achievement, and community impact.

**Examples:**
- "We're absolutely stoked to share some exciting news -- Trentham Electrical & Solar has won @aikoenergy Best Residential Installation!"
- "Another future-proof install we're proud to hand over."
- "It's that mix of craftsmanship and pride that makes our installs stand out."
- "We're proud of the systems we install -- but we're even prouder of the crew behind them."

**Frequency:** Safe to use in almost every post. Tone should be earned pride, not boastfulness.

### 6.2 WARMTH (present in ~60% of posts)

Community connection, team spirit, personal touches. The "neighbour" feeling.

**Examples:**
- "We love being part of projects that invest in our community and our kids."
- "At Trentham Electrical & Solar, we're your neighbours -- not just your installers."
- "When you choose us, you're not just investing in solar -- you're supporting local jobs."
- "Meet Sam -- the newest member of our team! Sam's a down-to-earth A Grade Electrician who loves keeping his mind and body active."

**Frequency:** Present in most posts as an undertone. Dial up for team and community content.

### 6.3 ENTHUSIASM (present in ~40% of posts)

Genuine excitement about technology, big installs, and industry developments. Never fake or forced.

**Examples:**
- "And he's still buzzing from it."
- "The team was seriously impressed with the Fronius Verto's capabilities."
- "Love seeing him dive deep into the nerdy side of solar."
- "Australia's First Fronius Verto Inverter Install!"

**Frequency:** Use for product launches, award wins, large installs, industry firsts. Not appropriate for every post -- save it for genuinely exciting content.

### 6.4 URGENCY (occasional -- ~10% of posts)

Used exclusively for rebate deadlines and time-sensitive government incentive changes. Always factual, never manufactured.

**Examples:**
- "Don't let the May 1st rebate 'cliff' catch you off guard."
- "The clock is ticking."
- "Don't wait for the next blackout."
- "Get in now to have your system ready."
- "The difference between installing in April versus June could cost you several thousand dollars."

**Frequency:** Only when there is a genuine deadline. Never fabricate urgency. Even urgent posts maintain the measured, trustworthy tone.

### 6.5 HUMOUR (light, infrequent -- ~5% of posts)

Gentle, dad-joke level humour. Puns, light wordplay. Never at anyone's expense.

**Examples:**
- "We can't roo-sist sharing this one..." (kangaroo pun at wildlife sanctuary install)
- "Should call them LONG Blacks!" (LONGi panels at coffee roasters)
- "If your business wants to stay out of the rough..." (golf club install)
- "Yes, there may have been a few 'roo' puns thrown around on-site."

**Frequency:** Sparingly. One humorous post per 15-20 posts. Humour should arise naturally from the project or situation, never forced.

### 6.6 EDUCATIONAL (present in ~25% of posts)

Confidence in explaining technical concepts clearly. The "knowledgeable mate" tone.

**Examples:**
- "Modern panels work in the shade and overcast at a reduced capacity, and modern inverters have algorithms to deal with shading."
- "The Victorian government's battery rebate isn't a straightforward cash-back deal. Instead, it works through something called Small-scale Technology Certificates (STCs)."
- "DC hybrids connect solar panels and batteries directly to the inverter."
- "Let's be honest: navigating government rebate programs can feel like wading through mud."

**Frequency:** Appropriate for blog posts, educational Instagram posts, product spotlights, rebate explainers. Always pair technical information with plain-English translation.

## 8. Visual Identity

### 8.1 Brand Colour: Purple

Purple is the defining visual element of Trentham Electrical & Solar. The purple vans, purple accents on uniforms, and purple heart emoji in social content create instant brand recognition in the region. Purple is distinctive in the trades world -- it separates Trentham from the typical blue, red, or yellow tradesperson branding.

**How purple appears:** Fully wrapped purple vans with logo, phone number, website, and QR code. Dark charcoal/grey polo shirts and hoodies with purple-accented Trentham Electrical & Solar logo (a tree/circuit board design). Purple dress worn to award ceremonies. Purple heart emoji used in social content.

### 8.2 What Gets Photographed

| Subject | Frequency | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Aerial/drone shots of completed rooftop installs | ~45% | The signature image. Black panels on rooftops against green Macedon Ranges landscape. |
| Team members at work or portraits | ~15% | On-roof shots, green foliage backgrounds, natural light, candid poses. B&W artistic portraits from late 2024 onward. |
| Scenic landscape with solar | ~10% | Sweeping views of bushland, farms, hills. The setting is always part of the story. |
| Equipment close-ups (inverters, batteries) | ~10% | Fronius white boxes, Victron blue gear, BYD batteries, tidy wiring. |
| Purple vans | ~8% | Sometimes the main subject, sometimes in background of install shots. Recurring visual motif. |
| Events/conferences | ~5% | All Energy booth photos, award ceremonies. |
| Graphics/designed images | ~5% | Rebate announcements, infographics. Less common. |
| Miscellaneous | ~2% | Baby kangaroos, Christmas photos, app screenshots. |

### 8.3 Composition Patterns

- **Aerial/bird's-eye (dominant):** Drone shots at 30-60 degree angle above the roof, showing panels in context with surrounding landscape. This is the visual signature.
- **Wide establishing shots:** Properties shown in full landscape context -- rolling hills, eucalyptus forests, country roads. The setting is always part of the story.
- **Portrait-style team photos:** Natural outdoor settings, natural light, casual and approachable poses. Never stiff or corporate. B&W artistic photography (silhouettes, moody interiors) introduced from late 2024.
- **Close-ups are rare:** When they appear (wiring, inverters), they show tidy, professional work.

### 8.4 Branding Visibility Rules

- **Purple vans** should be visible in 20-25% of all images. They appear as deliberate subjects and incidentally in backgrounds.
- **Branded uniforms:** Team members always wear dark charcoal/grey Trentham Electrical & Solar branded polo shirts or hoodies.
- **Branded caps:** Mick frequently wears a cap with the brand logo.
- **No branded stickers on installs:** The installations themselves do not carry visible branding.
- **Mick's appearance:** Full beard, cap, branded shirt, sunglasses. Recognisable and consistent.

### 8.5 Photo Quality Standards

- **Minimum standard:** Clear, well-lit, intentionally composed. No blurry, poorly cropped, or harsh-lighting images.
- **Target standard:** Professional drone shots with landscape context. Dramatic sky conditions (clouds over ranges, golden light). Properties with visual interest (pools, architectural features, rural settings).

## 10. Do's and Don'ts

### 10.1 DO's

1. **DO use "we/our" consistently.** The brand speaks as a team: "We're proud of the systems we install." Never use "I" in brand content (exception: direct Mick quotes in editorial contexts).

2. **DO end every post with a soft CTA.** "Thinking about solar for your home? Let's chat." Never leave a post without an invitation.

3. **DO include system specs for install showcase posts.** Always list kW size, panel count and brand, inverter brand and model, battery capacity (if applicable). Format as emoji-bulleted lists.

4. **DO name specific towns and suburbs.** "Another stunning install in Kyneton" is always better than "another install in the region."

5. **DO celebrate completed work as craftsmanship.** "Our crew put real care into every detail -- from the layout of the panels to the tidy wiring behind the scenes."

6. **DO use the triple-rhythm list pattern.** "Performance, reliability, and future-proof energy independence." Groups of three are a brand fingerprint.

7. **DO use em-dashes for conversational asides.** "It's about future-proofing your home -- while keeping it looking sharp."

8. **DO open with a punchy statement or question.** "Power costs hurting your bottom line?" or "Another powerhouse install complete!"

9. **DO name team members with personal details.** "Meet Sam -- the newest member of our team! Sam's a down-to-earth A Grade Electrician."

10. **DO use storytelling hooks.** "Sometimes a job leads to the next one just down the street." Stories are more engaging than spec lists alone.

11. **DO translate technical jargon into plain language.** "Think of STCs as credits that get assigned to your battery system."

12. **DO reference local businesses when showcasing their installs.** Tag them on social media. It builds community goodwill.

13. **DO acknowledge challenges honestly.** "Not an easy roof to work on but we got there!" builds more trust than pretending every job is effortless.

14. **DO include the contact block on Instagram.** Website (www.trenthamelectrical.com) and phone (1300 3458 00) at the end of every post.

15. **DO use rhetorical questions to draw readers in.** "If you've ever looked at your roof and thought, 'Surely we could be doing something smarter here' -- you're probably right."

16. **DO frame environmental benefit as secondary.** Lead with savings, independence, or reliability. Sustainability is the quiet bonus.

17. **DO use brand-signature vocabulary.** "Powerhouse," "future-proof," "sleek," "game changer," "seriously," "tidy," "top-notch."

### 10.2 DON'Ts

1. **DON'T use "I" or "they" to refer to the business.** Always "we." Exception: direct Mick quotes in editorial pieces.

2. **DON'T lead with price.** Value is framed as long-term investment and independence, not cost. "It's not just about cutting bills."

3. **DON'T name competitors.** Differentiate through implied quality: "We've seen some horror stories from homeowners who went with out-of-town installers."

4. **DON'T use aggressive sales language.** No "Act now!", "Limited time!", "You can't afford to miss this!" Even rebate deadline posts maintain a measured tone.

5. **DON'T use corporate jargon.** No "synergy," "leverage," "stakeholder," "ecosystem," "paradigm."

6. **DON'T post without structure.** Use line breaks, emoji bullets, and short paragraphs. Walls of text are off-brand.

7. **DON'T use "customers" or "clients" in public content.** Address as "you," "homeowners," "neighbours," or by first name.

8. **DON'T be preachy about the environment.** No climate guilt, no activist language, no political framing of renewable energy.

9. **DON'T use more than one or two Australian slang terms per post.** Casual-professional, not excessively colloquial.

10. **DON'T promise specific outcomes without context.** "Estimated annual savings: $1,726.91" (tied to a specific system) is fine. "You'll save thousands!" (vague promise) is not.

11. **DON'T copy-paste identical hashtag blocks.** Tailor hashtags to each post's content, location, and products.

12. **DON'T post purely promotional content without narrative or educational value.** Every post should teach, tell a story, or celebrate -- not just advertise.

13. **DON'T use stock photo aesthetic.** All imagery should feel authentic, on-site, and real.

14. **DON'T skip the human element.** Even spec-heavy posts should include a narrative about the property, customer, or team.

15. **DON'T use emojis excessively on GBP.** GBP posts use 0-5 emojis. Instagram uses more (5-15). Match the platform.

16. **DON'T use the word "cheap" or "budget" to describe Trentham's work.** "Fair pricing" (from a customer review) is the ceiling for price language.

17. **DON'T over-promise on complex off-grid project timelines.** Frame these projects honestly, including the design complexity involved.
