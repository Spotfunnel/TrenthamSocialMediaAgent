# Blog Guide -- Trentham Electrical & Solar

**Platform:** Wix Blog
**Content types:** Full educational articles (600-1200 words) and job spotlight posts (200-400 words)
**Operator:** AI agent (OpenAI) generating drafts autonomously
**Voice baseline:** Builds on the Master Brand Guide. Blog is the most structured, most informative channel -- but the same personality runs underneath.

---

## 1. Article Templates

### 1.1 Full Educational Article (600-1200 words)

The Solar Battery Rebate blog post is the gold standard. Every educational article should follow its architecture.

**Structural outline:**

| Section | Heading type | Word target | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Title | H1 | 8-15 words | SEO keyword + location + year where relevant |
| Opening hook | Body text | 60-100 words | Identify the reader's situation, establish timeliness, hint at value |
| Section 1 | H2 | 80-150 words | Define the core concept in plain English |
| Section 2 | H2 | 80-150 words | Explain why it matters right now (deadline, cost, regulation change) |
| Section 3 | H2 | 100-180 words | Practical detail -- sizing, eligibility, specs |
| Section 4 | H2 | 80-150 words | Qualification requirements or step-by-step process |
| Section 5 | H2 | 80-120 words | How Trentham Electrical handles this for the reader |
| Section 6 (optional) | H2 | 60-100 words | Related opportunity (loans, additional rebates) |
| Section 7 | H2 | 80-120 words | The application or installation process |
| Closing section | H2 | 80-120 words | Decision framing + soft CTA |

**Heading hierarchy rules:**
- H1: Title only. One per post. Never repeated in body.
- H2: Every major section. 6-8 per educational article.
- H3: Use sparingly for sub-points within a section. Never skip from H1 to H3.
- No H4 or deeper in blog content.

**Opening pattern:**
Start with the reader's situation, not the topic definition. The rebate guide opens with "If you've been sitting on the fence about adding home battery storage to your solar setup, 2026 might be your best opportunity yet." This works because it speaks to indecision (a real feeling) before diving into policy detail.

**Body structure:**
Move from concept to urgency to practical detail to process to CTA. Each section should answer one question. Never pack two topics into one H2 section.

**Conclusion/CTA pattern:**
Always end with a warm, personal invitation. Frame it as a conversation, not a transaction. The gold standard closing: "reach out to us at Trentham Electrical & Solar. We'll come out, have a look at your setup, and give you straight answers about what makes sense for your situation -- no pressure, no BS. Just honest advice from locals who've been doing this work across Central Victoria for years."

### 1.2 Job Spotlight Post (200-400 words)

Job spotlights are longer and more narrative than Instagram install posts. Where Instagram celebrates the result in 150 words with emoji-bulleted specs, the blog tells the story of the project -- the challenge, the solution, and the outcome.

**Structural outline:**

| Section | Heading type | Word target | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Title | H1 | 6-12 words | Project type + distinguishing feature or partner brand |
| Opening narrative | Body text | 60-100 words | Set the scene: the property, the challenge, or the customer's need |
| Solution and specs | Body text | 80-150 words | What was installed and why those choices were made |
| Outcome or highlight | Body text | 40-80 words | Results, partnership value, or what makes this project notable |
| Closing line | Body text | 20-40 words | Brand statement, team appreciation, or forward-looking sentiment |

**How it differs from an Instagram install post:**
- No emoji bullets for specs. Weave specs into narrative sentences.
- No hashtags.
- Explain the "why" behind product choices, not just the "what."
- Include design rationale: "The FranklinWH battery, chosen for its off-grid certification, warranty, and generator input capabilities, became the heart of the solution."
- Can name the customer or location by first name if permission exists.

**Reference posts:**
- FranklinWH blog: Opens with project context, names the partnership, explains the custom software solution, closes with a brand pride statement. (~250 words)
- Highlight blog: Opens with "Challenging the Curve!" as an informal subtitle, describes the technical challenge of a curved roof, lists key specs in prose, closes with team appreciation. (~150 words -- on the short end; aim for 250-400.)

## 2. Full Annotated Example: Solar Battery Rebate Blog

Below is the structure of the rebate guide blog with annotations explaining why each element works.

**H1: "How to Maximize the Solar Battery Rebate in Victoria (2026 Guide)"**
> WHY: Follows the How-to + keyword + location + year formula. "Solar battery rebate Victoria" is the primary search term. The year signals freshness to both readers and search engines.

**Opening paragraph (no heading):**
> "If you've been sitting on the fence about adding home battery storage to your solar setup, 2026 might be your best opportunity yet."

> WHY: Speaks directly to the reader's state of mind ("sitting on the fence"), not to the topic. Creates immediate relevance. Does not open with a definition or a statistic.

**H2: "Understanding the Solar Battery Rebate Victoria Program"**
> Explains STCs in plain English. Uses the "term + plain English" pattern: "Think of STCs as credits that get assigned to your battery system based on its storage capacity."

> WHY: First H2 defines the concept for readers who arrived via search. Keyword "solar battery rebate Victoria" appears naturally. Jargon is translated immediately.

**H2: "Why the May 1 Deadline Matters More Than You Think"**
> Creates urgency through factual deadlines, not marketing pressure. "The difference between installing in April versus June could cost you several thousand dollars in lost rebate value."

> WHY: Urgency section comes second -- after the reader understands what the rebate is, they learn why timing matters. The heading uses "More Than You Think" to counter assumptions.

**H2: "Choosing the Right Battery Size for Maximum Rebate"**
> Makes numbers tangible: "So if you install a 10 kWh battery, you're looking at around $3,100 to $3,360 in rebate value." Uses bold-label list format for battery size tiers.

> WHY: Practical, decision-enabling section. Readers can see themselves in a specific scenario. Numbers build trust -- vague claims do the opposite.

**H2: "What You Need to Qualify for the Rebate"**
> Bold-label list format: "Approved Battery System:", "Accredited Installer:", "VPP Capability:" Each label followed by a plain-English explanation.

> WHY: Scannable format. Readers checking eligibility can find their answer without reading every word. Bold labels act as mini-headings within the section.

**H2: "How Trentham Electrical & Solar Makes This Process Simple"**
> Shifts from education to soft sell. "We come to your property, look at your existing setup." Names specific towns: "We've installed enough systems around Blackwood, Malmsbury, and Macedon."

> WHY: This is where the brand earns the right to sell -- after 800+ words of genuine education. Local name-dropping builds credibility. The tone stays advisory, not promotional.

**H2: "Don't Forget About Interest-Free Loans"**
> Adds value by covering a related opportunity the reader might not know about.

> WHY: Shows breadth of knowledge. Positions the business as a full-service guide, not just an installer.

**H2: "The Application Process (And How We Handle It)"**
> Walks through the Solar Victoria portal process. "This is the bit everyone dreads" breaks the formal tone with Mick's conversational voice.

> WHY: Demystifies bureaucracy. The parenthetical "(And How We Handle It)" signals that the business removes the hassle.

**H2: "Making Your Decision Before the Clock Runs Out"**
> Closing section. Returns to urgency with factual framing. CTA: "reach out to us... no pressure, no BS."

> WHY: Bookends the article by returning to the time-sensitivity established in H2 #2. CTA is warm and personal, not transactional.

## 3. Tone Calibration

### 3.1 How Blog Voice Differs from Social

The blog is the most structured and informative channel. It educates and explains where Instagram celebrates and promotes. But the same personality -- knowledgeable local tradie who communicates clearly -- runs through both. The key shift is register, not identity.

**Blog characteristics:**
- Medium-length paragraphs (3-5 sentences) with clear topic sentences
- Explanatory passages that build an argument across multiple sentences
- Self-deprecating honesty: "Let's be honest: navigating government rebate programs can feel like wading through mud."
- First-person plural ("we") with direct reader address ("you/your")
- No emoji. No hashtags. No exclamation-heavy celebration.

**Instagram characteristics (for contrast):**
- Single-sentence paragraphs separated by line breaks
- Emoji-bulleted spec lists
- Exclamatory celebration: "Another beauty on the books!"
- Hashtag blocks of 15-25 tags
- Punchy, energetic, visually driven

### 3.2 Side-by-Side Phrase Comparisons

| Context | Blog phrasing | Instagram equivalent |
|---------|--------------|---------------------|
| Opening a rebate topic | "If you've been sitting on the fence about adding home battery storage to your solar setup, 2026 might be your best opportunity yet." | "Don't let the May 1st rebate 'cliff' catch you off guard." |
| Describing an install | "The FranklinWH battery, chosen for its off-grid certification, warranty, and generator input capabilities, became the heart of the solution." | "Another solid solar + battery install ticked off. This home is now running a 12.32kW solar system paired with 16.12kWh of battery storage." |
| Explaining solar value | "For most households in Central Victoria, a 10-15 kWh battery hits the sweet spot between cost, practicality, and rebate value." | "More solar. More stored power. Less reliance on the grid." |
| Closing with CTA | "Reach out to us at Trentham Electrical & Solar. We'll come out, have a look at your setup, and give you straight answers about what makes sense for your situation -- no pressure, no BS." | "Thinking about solar for your home? Let's chat." |
| Expressing brand pride | "Trentham Electrical and Solar is proud to work alongside innovative partners like FranklinWH to deliver customized, resilient energy systems." | "We're absolutely stoked to share some exciting news!" |
| Discussing quality | "No cutting corners, no 'she'll be right' jobs." | "No hard sell, no fluff -- just solid, well-designed solar that works." |

### 3.3 Where Conversational "Mick Voice" Should Break Through

Even in the most structured educational article, the conversational voice must surface at key moments. These are the pressure-release valves that keep the blog from reading like a government brochure.

**Approved conversational break-throughs:**
- "no pressure, no BS. Just honest advice from locals" -- in closing CTAs
- "Let's be honest:" -- to introduce a difficult or confusing topic
- "Here's the kicker:" -- to highlight a surprising or important detail
- "This is the bit everyone dreads" -- to acknowledge bureaucratic pain points
- "No cutting corners, no 'she'll be right' jobs" -- to signal workmanship standards
- "you're literally leaving thousands of dollars on the table" -- to make inaction feel tangible

**Placement rule:** Deploy one conversational break-through per 200-300 words. They should feel like a mate leaning in to give you the real story, not like a comedian interrupting a lecture.

## 4. Technical Simplification Guide

### 4.1 The Pattern

Mick uses a consistent "term + plain English" pattern: introduce the technical term, then immediately translate it. Never assume the reader knows industry jargon. Never skip the translation.

### 4.2 Simplification Examples (Verbatim from Blog Content)

1. **STCs (Small-scale Technology Certificates):**
   "Instead, it works through something called Small-scale Technology Certificates (STCs). Think of STCs as credits that get assigned to your battery system based on its storage capacity."

2. **VPP (Virtual Power Plant):**
   "Your battery system needs to have the technical ability to connect to a Virtual Power Plant. This doesn't mean it has to be connected right away -- it just needs to have the capability."

3. **Compliance certificates:**
   "The system is considered 'installed' when we sign off on the electrical compliance certificate -- that's the official trigger point."

4. **STC market rates:**
   "Those STCs translate to real money: roughly $310 to $336 per kWh of battery storage at current market rates."

5. **kWh made tangible:**
   "So if you install a 10 kWh battery, you're looking at around $3,100 to $3,360 in rebate value. Scale that up to the maximum eligible capacity of 50 kWh, and you're talking about $15,540 in potential savings."

6. **Off-grid certification:**
   "The FranklinWH battery, chosen for its off-grid certification, warranty, and generator input capabilities, became the heart of the solution."

7. **Optimisers:**
   "18.5kW of LONGi All Black Panels fitted with optimisers to ensure maximum efficiency, all installed seamlessly on the roof's unique curve."

8. **DC-coupled vs AC-coupled:**
   "DC hybrids connect solar panels and batteries directly to the inverter. During a power outage, a DC hybrid system will continue to generate electricity."

9. **Feed-in tariff:**
   When referencing the feed-in tariff, explain it as the rate the grid pays you for excess solar power sent back.

10. **Powercor data:**
    "We log into the Powercor portal" -- explain this as the electricity distributor's usage records that show exactly how much power the home uses and when.

11. **SAA accreditation:**
    "Your installer must be SAA accredited (this replaced the old CEC accreditation)." -- Always note the name change so readers searching for "CEC accredited installer" understand the current term.

12. **VEECs (Victorian Energy Efficiency Certificates):**
    When referencing VEECs, explain them as government incentive certificates for energy-efficient upgrades like heat pumps, similar to how STCs work for solar.

### 4.3 Making Numbers Tangible

Never present a number without context. Always pair a figure with a scenario the reader can picture:

- "10 kWh battery = $3,100-$3,360 in rebate value"
- "Scale that up to the maximum eligible capacity of 50 kWh, and you're talking about $15,540 in potential savings"
- "The difference between installing in April versus June could cost you several thousand dollars"
- "4-8 weeks" for processing time -- give this as a planning horizon, not just a stat
- "batteries between 5 kWh and 100 kWh" -- show the range so readers can place themselves within it

## 5. SEO Checklist

### 5.1 Title Format

- **Educational guides:** How-to + primary keyword + location + year
  - Example: "How to Maximize the Solar Battery Rebate in Victoria (2026 Guide)"
- **Job spotlights:** Company name + action verb + descriptive project + partner brand (optional)
  - Example: "Trentham Electrical and Solar Completes Innovative Off-Grid Project with FranklinWH Custom Solution"
- **Community news:** Community topic + location + news hook
  - Example: "Community Battery Hopes Spark in Trentham with Grant Application"

### 5.2 Keyword Placement Map

| Location | Rule |
|----------|------|
| H1 title | Primary keyword must appear. Place it early. |
| First paragraph | Primary keyword within the first two sentences. |
| H2 headings | Include the primary keyword or a close variant in at least 2 of the H2s. Remaining H2s should use related/secondary keywords. |
| Body text | Primary keyword appears 4-6 times across the full article. Do not force it -- if it reads unnaturally, use a variant. |
| Conclusion section | Primary keyword appears once in the closing paragraph. |
| Image alt text | Descriptive alt text that naturally includes the keyword where relevant. |

### 5.3 Meta Description Template

60-155 characters. Structure: [Value proposition] + [primary keyword] + [location signal] + [CTA or differentiator].

Example: "Learn how to maximise Victoria's solar battery rebate in 2026. Local Hepburn Shire installers explain STCs, eligibility, and deadlines."

### 5.4 Slug Format

- Lowercase, hyphenated, keyword-first
- Remove filler words (the, a, an, and, in, of) unless they improve readability
- Examples:
  - `solar-battery-rebate-victoria-2026-guide`
  - `off-grid-project-franklinwh-custom-solution`
  - `community-battery-trentham-grant-application`

### 5.5 Image Requirements

- Every blog post must have a featured image.
- Every image must have descriptive alt text.
- Educational articles: 2-3 images minimum (hero + 1-2 supporting).
- Job spotlights: 1-2 images (installation photo required).
- Alt text example: "Aerial view of rural property with solar panel arrays in Central Victoria"

## 6. CTA Integration

### 6.1 The Principle

CTAs are framed as conversations, not sales. The reader should feel invited, not pressured. Education earns the right to sell -- a CTA should only appear after genuine value has been delivered.

### 6.2 Transition Pattern

Move from educational content to CTA by bridging with a "how we help" section. Never jump from explaining a concept directly to "call us now." The bridge section should describe what happens when the reader reaches out -- what the experience feels like.

### 6.3 Real CTA Examples for Blog Context

1. **The gold standard (warm invitation):**
   "Reach out to us at Trentham Electrical & Solar. We'll come out, have a look at your setup, and give you straight answers about what makes sense for your situation -- no pressure, no BS. Just honest advice from locals who've been doing this work across Central Victoria for years."

2. **The process demystifier:**
   "We handle the Solar Victoria portal application, the compliance paperwork, and the STC process. You just need to own the property and give us the go-ahead."

3. **The local trust signal:**
   "When you work with a local team like ours, you've got people you can actually reach when you need help."

4. **The low-commitment opener:**
   "If you're not sure whether a battery makes sense for your setup, we're happy to have a down-to-earth chat about how storage works for your property."

5. **The urgency-with-honesty framing:**
   "The difference between installing in April versus June could cost you several thousand dollars in lost rebate value. And once that rate drops, it's not coming back. This is a genuine use-it-or-lose-it situation."

6. **The brand pride close:**
   "Trentham Electrical and Solar is proud to work alongside innovative partners like FranklinWH to deliver customized, resilient energy systems."

### 6.4 Competitor Differentiation Without Naming Competitors

Never name a competitor. Instead, describe the negative outcome of choosing poorly and let the reader draw conclusions:

- "We've seen some horror stories from homeowners who went with out-of-town installers chasing cheap prices. They got dodgy installs, batteries that don't work properly, and no one to call when things go wrong."
- "When you work with a local team like ours, you've got people you can actually reach when you need help."
- "No cutting corners, no 'she'll be right' jobs."

The pattern is: describe the bad experience (without naming who delivers it) then contrast with what Trentham Electrical does differently.

## 7. Credibility Markers

### 7.1 Accreditations -- When and How to Cite

- **SAA accreditation:** Cite when discussing installer qualifications or rebate eligibility. "Your installer must be SAA accredited (this replaced the old CEC accreditation). At Trentham Electrical & Solar, we hold all the necessary accreditations."
- **CEC (Clean Energy Council):** Reference in About-style content or when discussing system design standards. "We're proud to be Clean Energy Council certified system designers and installers."
- **Registered Electrical Contractor (22757):** Cite when discussing compliance, safety, or regulatory topics.
- Do not list all accreditations in every post. Choose the one most relevant to the article topic.

### 7.2 Regulatory References

- **Solar Victoria:** Reference when discussing rebates, the portal application process, or eligibility criteria.
- **STCs (Small-scale Technology Certificates):** Explain fully on first mention in any article. Can abbreviate on subsequent mentions.
- **VEECs (Victorian Energy Efficiency Certificates):** Reference for heat pump and energy efficiency content.
- **Energy Safe Victoria (ESV):** Reference only when discussing compliance and safety standards.
- Always cite current rates and dates. If an STC rate or deadline has changed, the article must be updated.

### 7.3 Local Knowledge Signals

Weave in specific geographic references that only a local business would know:

- "We've installed enough systems around Blackwood, Malmsbury, and Macedon to know what works in our local conditions"
- "For homeowners across the Hepburn and Macedon Ranges regions"
- Reference specific local conditions: the cooler Central Highlands climate, shading from eucalyptus, rural properties with long driveways, off-grid needs in remote areas
- Name local towns served: Bacchus Marsh, Ballan, Blackwood, Castlemaine, Daylesford, Gisborne, Hepburn, Kyneton, Macedon, Tylden, Woodend, and others across the service area

### 7.4 Specific Data Points for Trust

Use precise numbers, never vague claims:

- "$310 to $336 per kWh" (not "hundreds of dollars per kWh")
- "4-8 weeks" (not "a few weeks")
- "batteries between 5 kWh and 100 kWh" (not "a range of battery sizes")
- "25-year panel life" (not "decades of performance")
- Estimated annual savings with dollar figures when available
- ROI percentages from real installs when available

## 8. Internal Linking Rules

### 8.1 Pages to Link To

- **Contact page:** Link from every CTA section. Anchor text should vary: "reach out to us", "get in touch", "contact the team."
- **Service pages:** Link when discussing a specific service (grid-connected solar, off-grid, batteries, heat pumps, EV charging). Use the service name as anchor text.
- **Location pages:** Link when mentioning a specific town or region. Anchor text: the town name itself (e.g., "solar installations in Woodend").
- **Other blog posts:** Cross-link between related blog posts. If a rebate guide mentions battery sizing, link to a battery explainer post if one exists.

### 8.2 Anchor Text Patterns

- Use descriptive anchor text, never "click here" or "read more."
- Vary anchor text across posts to avoid repetitive patterns.
- Examples: "solar battery rebate in Victoria", "our off-grid solar solutions", "contact us for a site assessment"

### 8.3 Frequency Recommendations

- Educational articles (600-1200 words): 3-5 internal links
- Job spotlight posts (200-400 words): 1-2 internal links
- Every post must link to the contact page at least once.
- Never link the same page more than twice in a single post.

## 9. Paragraph and Formatting Rules

### 9.1 Paragraph Structure

- 3-5 sentences per paragraph. No single-sentence paragraphs (that is Instagram style).
- Each paragraph should address one idea with a clear topic sentence.
- White space between every paragraph -- no wall-of-text blocks.
- Vary paragraph length. A run of identically-sized paragraphs feels monotonous.

### 9.2 Bold-Label List Format

For qualification requirements, feature lists, or process steps, use the bold-label format:

**Approved Battery System:** The battery must be on the Clean Energy Council's approved product list.
**Accredited Installer:** Your installer must be SAA accredited.
**VPP Capability:** Your battery system needs to have the technical ability to connect to a Virtual Power Plant.

This format is scannable, works well on mobile, and mirrors how the rebate guide blog structures its key information.

### 9.3 When to Use Bullet Points vs Prose

- **Use bullet points** for: spec lists, qualification criteria, step-by-step processes, feature comparisons.
- **Use prose** for: narrative explanations, storytelling, opinion, CTA sections.
- Never use emoji as bullet markers in blog posts. Use standard HTML bullets or the bold-label format.

### 9.4 Heading Frequency

- One H2 every 100-200 words in educational articles.
- Job spotlight posts may use zero H2s if under 300 words (the FranklinWH and Highlight posts use no H2s). For posts closer to 400 words, one or two H2s improve scannability.

## 11. What Blog Posts Never Do

1. **No emoji in blog text.** Emoji are for Instagram. Blog posts use standard punctuation, bold text, and bullet points for visual structure.

2. **No hashtags.** Hashtags serve no SEO or readability purpose on a Wix blog. They are an Instagram convention only.

3. **No copy-pasting social media captions as blog content.** A blog post and an Instagram caption about the same install must be written separately. Blog content is longer, more narrative, and structured with headings. An Instagram caption dropped into a blog post reads shallow and unfinished.

4. **No publishing without heading structure.** Every educational article must have an H1 title and multiple H2 sections. Even short job spotlight posts must have a properly formatted H1 title.

5. **No aggressive urgency language.** "Act now!", "Limited time only!", "Don't miss out!" are never used. Factual deadlines are fine: "The STC rate drops on May 1" is a fact. "HURRY BEFORE IT'S TOO LATE!" is marketing pressure and is off-brand.

6. **No naming competitors.** Differentiation is done through describing bad outcomes of poor installation practices and contrasting with what Trentham Electrical does. Never name another business.

7. **No unsubstantiated claims.** Every number must be current and verifiable. Every accreditation must be one the business actually holds. Do not claim "best in Victoria" or "cheapest in the region."

8. **No walls of text.** Every blog post must have white space between paragraphs, headings to break up sections, and scannable formatting (bold labels, bullet points where appropriate).

9. **No stock marketing phrases.** "Synergy," "leverage," "holistic solution," "end-to-end," "one-stop shop" -- these are corporate cliches. Write like a knowledgeable local explaining things over coffee.

10. **No publishing without a CTA.** Every blog post, regardless of type, must end with a way for the reader to take the next step -- a link to the contact page, a phone number, or an invitation to get in touch.
